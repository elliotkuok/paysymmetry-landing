FAQ

There are tons of great reasons to share your salary with coworkers, but we focus one two big ones: negotiating higher fair wages and recognizing unfair ones.

The short answer is no, it is not against the law to tell your own salary to anyone else.
In fact, there are actually laws in place that protect your right to share your salary information with others. According to the National Labor Relations Act, employees of most companies have the right to engage in “concerted activity for mutual aid or protection” and the right to “organize a union to negotiate with [their] employer concerning [their] wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment.” The National Labor Relations Board, which enforces the Act, has repeatedly held that this right to organize includes the right to discuss your pay with coworkers.
But the long answer is that you still need to be cautious.
Sharing your salary is not against the law, but doing so could violate a contract you signed with your employer, and that in turn is against the law under normal circumstances. If the contract is itself worded illegally (for example if it says “you may not discuss your salary with your coworkers” and the company is subject to the NLRA) then it is unenforceable, but that doesn’t mean your employer won’t try to enforce it.
Moreover, the National Labor Relations Act will protect you if you’re only sharing your salary with coworkers, but it doesn’t apply if you are sharing your salary with the general public, or for reasons other than organizing/negotiating.
That’s why we try so hard to keep everything anonymous on this site.

No one will be able to see your salary until three people have entered salary information. We do this so that the group creator (by default the first person to enter info) can’t be identified as the creator by the second person to join, even if the creator chooses to use their own name in the invite email.

Once you invite coworkers to your group or join someone else’s group, all that the other group members will be able to see is your job title and your salary. We will never show them this information until at least one other person of your job title have also joined the group. This is so that your salary can’t be easily identified by the person who invited you. (This is also why we say in the email invite how many people have been invited to the group – so that the group creator can’t invite one target individual and learn their salary.)
That said, it’s important to remember that your coworkers know a lot about you, and they may be able to guess which salary is yours using information that they don’t get from PaySymmetry. For example, if you have been working much longer than all the other people with your job title, your salary is probably higher and they may be able to reasonably deduce that the highest salary they see associated with that job title is yours.
We think that it’s a risk worth taking. PaySymmetry is about using higher quality information to negotiate your salary, but it’s also about breaking down the mental barriers we all have about discussing pay, and part of that exercise is sometimes accepting less than complete anonymity.

Your boss pays you, so odds are they know how much money you make. If you work with a large cohort that all have the similar salaries and the same job title, this may not be a problem. But if salaries vary in your field or there aren’t many people at your company with your job title, they may see your salary next to your job title and be able to figure out that you are the one who posted it. Of course we will never reveal your account information so they won’t be able to conclusively prove that you posted it, but it could still get you into some hot water. Luckily, there is an easy solution…
Do not invite your boss. Tell your coworkers not to invite your boss. Don’t invite anyone who isn’t your coworker. That way no one will invite your boss.
