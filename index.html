About

Our goal is to help you negotiate for a better salary and recognize pay discrimination by giving you the tools to anonymously share salary information with your coworkers. We hope to promote solidarity among workers in all industries, especially those not traditionally served by unions.

Whether you’re trying to negotiate a raise, investigate discrimination or organize with your coworkers, you have the right to share salary information with your colleagues according to the National Labor Relations Act.

The short answer is no, it is not against the law to tell your own salary to anyone else.
In fact, there are actually laws in place that protect your right to share your salary information with others. According to the National Labor Relations Act, employees of most companies have the right to engage in “concerted activity for mutual aid or protection” and the right to “organize a union to negotiate with [their] employer concerning [their] wages, hours, and other terms and conditions of employment.” The National Labor Relations Board, which enforces the Act, has repeatedly held that this right to organize includes the right to discuss your pay with coworkers.
But the long answer is that you still need to be cautious.
Sharing your salary is not against the law, but doing so could violate a contract you signed with your employer, and that in turn is against the law under normal circumstances. If the contract is itself worded illegally (for example if it says “you may not discuss your salary with your coworkers” and the company is subject to the NLRA) then it is unenforceable, but that doesn’t mean your employer won’t try to enforce it.
Moreover, the National Labor Relations Act will protect you if you’re only sharing your salary with coworkers, but it doesn’t apply if you are sharing your salary with the general public, or for reasons other than organizing/negotiating.

According to recent studies, more than half of the American workforce and over 60% of the private sector work force is forbidden or discourage from discussing their salaries with coworkers, so you may see this type of language in your employment contract, or hear it expressed informally around the office.
There are a few things to consider if you’re in this situation: Your boss may simply be unaware of the laws protecting salary sharing, or they might be hoping that you’re unaware of the law, but either way you need to tread lightly.
If you have a great relationship with your boss, consider explaining the National Labor Relations Act to them and encouraging them to change the language in their contracts and the culture of secrecy they’re fostering. This might all be as new to them as it is to you.
If you have an iffy relationship with your boss, be wary. The bottom line is this: if your employer discovers that you have violated your contract, they may enforce it (fire you) even if doing so is illegal. Filing a claim with the National Labor Relations Board can take significant time and money, and it does not guarantee you’ll get your job back. It makes sense to air on the side of caution and anonymity.

It’s time to negotiate. This is what you’ve been training for! And it’s why you opted to share your salary in the first place.
Ask to meet with your boss, tell them you’d like a raise. Use the information you now have about your coworkers’ salaries to suggest a reasonable amount. If your boss says no, tell her you think the figure you suggested is in line with what your coworkers are making. Try not to blow the whole “anonymous” thing (e.g. “Helen told me she makes X” or even “We all decided to share our salary information on this really cool website, I’ll forward you the invite Helen sent me as proof”) but be assertive. Once you’ve double-checked that the NLRA applies to your company, don’t be afraid to let your boss know that you have been exercising your right to organize by discussing salary information.
